## Introduction

Fast fashion brands increasingly promote sustainability initiatives, but the extent to which these initiatives produce real environmental improvement remains unclear. This project combines a structured Kaggle dataset, Guardian news articles, Google Trends data, and manually reviewed relevant websites to examine both environmental performance and public-facing sustainability narratives. The analysis focuses on H&M, Zara, and Shein, comparing production, emissions, water consumption, textile waste, and the timing of green initiatives relative to external pressure.

## Description of Data and Data Sources

This project uses four main data sources:

- A structured Kaggle dataset: *The True Cost of Fast Fashion Impact*.
- Guardian news articles collected through the Guardian API to track pressure, scrutiny, and sustainability-related coverage.
- Google Trends data collected to measure search interest in green collections and seasonal sustainability-related attention.
- Relevant websites and sustainability pages were manually reviewed to document green initiative launches and publicly stated sustainability claims.

The Kaggle dataset provides brand-level environmental indicators such as production, carbon emissions, water consumption, and waste. The Guardian articles were used to identify when public pressure, criticism, and brand sustainability launches appeared in the media. Google Trends data was used to examine whether interest in green collections rises around key commercial periods such as Black Friday. Relevant website pages were manually reviewed, and the launch dates and sustainability claims were recorded into a structured format for comparison with external pressure events.

## Research Questions and Conclusions

### H1
Fast fashion brands publicly commit to reducing their environmental impact through sustainability targets and conscious collections, but production volumes, carbon emissions, and textile waste data show these promises are not being fulfilled.

### H2
While fast fashion brands sell to wealthy consumers, the toxic waste and pollution generated by the industry are disproportionately dumped in developing countries.

### H3
Fast fashion brands deploy sustainability initiatives as strategic reputation management under external scrutiny, but these initiatives are not associated with meaningful reductions in environmental harm and often coincide with continued or worsening impact.

### H3 Sub-hypotheses
- **H3.0:** If media scrutiny of greenwashing is increasing, then the number of Guardian articles about the fast fashion green initiative should rise over time.
- **H3.1:** Green initiatives are reactive, not proactive.
- **H3.2:** Despite green initiatives, environmental footprint got worse.
- **H3.3:** Green collection search interest rises near Black Friday, suggesting a sales-driven sustainability strategy.

## Methodology

### Cleaning
The dataset was filtered to keep only the three target brands: H&M, Zara, and Shein. Column names were checked and standardized so that the correct fields could be used for emissions, production, water use, and waste. For the H3 analysis, Guardian API article data was cleaned by keeping only relevant article records, then grouping them by date, brand, and topic so pressure events and sustainability launches could be compared consistently. Google Trends data for H3.3 was also cleaned and organized by date so search interest could be compared across key seasonal periods. Relevant brand website pages were manually reviewed, and the launch dates and sustainability claims were recorded into a structured format for comparison with external pressure events.

### Analysis
For H1 and H2, the analysis compares environmental indicators across brands and over time to test whether sustainability claims align with actual performance. For H3, the analysis compares the timing of external pressure with green initiative launches and then checks whether emissions, production, and media scrutiny change after launch. For H3.3, Google Trends was used to test whether search interest in green collections increases near Black Friday, which would suggest that sustainability messaging is tied to sales periods. For H3.1, website-based evidence was used to identify when brands publicly launched green initiatives relative to external pressure.

### Visualization
For H1 and H2, the visual focus is on the main environmental results, using trend-based and comparison-based charts. For H3, the visuals include a pressure-and-launch timeline, before-and-after line charts, and article-trend and search-interest plots to show how green initiatives relate to scrutiny and performance over time. Website-based evidence for H3.1 is shown through the launch timeline and associated brand claim annotations.

## Main Findings and Insights

The analysis suggests that sustainability messaging does not consistently correspond to real environmental improvement. In several cases, brands continued to produce heavily or maintain high emissions after launching green initiatives. The Guardian article timeline also suggests that sustainability initiatives often followed external scrutiny rather than leading it. Google Trends data further supports the idea that green collection interest is tied to commercial moments rather than consistent environmental commitment.

## Further Questions and Next Steps

Future work could expand the analysis to include more brands, longer time spans, or more granular country-level waste and pollution data. It would also be useful to compare official brand claims with independent environmental audits or NGO reports. Another next step would be to measure media tone more carefully to separate neutral coverage from critical greenwashing coverage.
